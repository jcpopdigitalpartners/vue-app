## Context

Wednesday at Metro Health System. Two cases land on the patient access coordinator’s desk:

**Case A — Mr. Okonkwo**, Deaf since birth, uses **American Sign Language**. He has pre-operative teaching for elective knee replacement Friday 2:00 PM. Scheduling booked a video visit because “it’s more convenient,” but the platform has no ASL interpreter button and the surgeon’s office told him to “read the captions.” He declined the visit and has not completed required anticoagulation teaching.

**Case B — Ms. Chen**, full-time wheelchair user after spinal cord injury, has a **PET-CT** ordered stat for suspected recurrence. Radiology is in the **1960s annex** — entrance has steps, no automatic door, and the PET table in that suite is fixed-height. Transport can get her to the wing, but techs say they “don’t have a lift policy” and suggest she “try the hospital across town.” Her oncologist’s note says “scan within 48 hours.”

Neither case is a clinical disagreement; both are **access failures** in the operational path to care.

## Questions

### access-q1

type: reflection

For each case, separate **physical**, **communication**, and **process** barriers. What should have happened at the moment of scheduling — not the day before the procedure?

> Hint: ASL interpreter requests, qualified interpreter vs family, accessible route survey, equipment capability, and written accommodation plans in the chart.

> Insight: Medical accessibility failures usually originate **upstream** when accommodations are treated as last-minute exceptions. Effective communication for Deaf patients requires **qualified interpreters** (live or VRI) requested with lead time — captions alone are often not equivalent. Imaging access requires confirming **room, table, and transfer capability** before promising an appointment, not after the patient arrives.

### access-q2

type: multiple_choice
correct: 3

Mr. Okonkwo’s office offered a video visit with captions instead of an in-person ASL interpreter. Why is this a common operations mistake?

1. Deaf patients always prefer video to in-person care
2. Captions satisfy federal effective-communication requirements in every clinical context
3. Captions may be **technically available** but fail **effective communication** for complex informed-consent teaching
4. Interpreters are only required in the emergency department

> Insight: **Accessibility is contextual.** Automated captions can work for brief logistics but often fail for nuanced pre-op education where questions, nuance, and consent matter. Operations teams should treat interpreter booking like **critical equipment** — reserved, confirmed, and documented — with the same seriousness as sterilizing instruments.

### access-q3

type: reflection

You chair a 45-minute huddle with scheduling, radiology, and surgical services. Name **one policy change** and **one metric** for each case that would prevent recurrence without shifting blame to front-desk staff.

> Hint: Accommodation flags in scheduling rules, accessible modality inventory, 48-hour interpreter SLA, and “appointment confirmed accessible” checklist.

> Insight: Durable fixes combine **rules in scheduling systems** (hard stops without accommodation fields completed) with **inventory visibility** (which suites are wheelchair- and lift-capable). Metrics might include **percent of high-risk accommodations confirmed 72 hours ahead**, **no-show due to access barrier**, and **time from order to completed scan for patients flagged mobility needs** — measuring whether the system delivered care, not whether staff “tried their best.”
